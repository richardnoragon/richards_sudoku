[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Kendoku_ans.jpg/250px-Kendoku_ans.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kendoku_ans.jpg)

A simple KenKen puzzle, with answers filled in as large numbers.

**KenKen** and **KenDoku** are trademarked names for a style of arithmetic and [logic puzzle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic_puzzle "Logic puzzle") invented in 2004 by Japanese math teacher [Tetsuya Miyamoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetsuya_Miyamoto "Tetsuya Miyamoto"),<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_2009.feb.08_1-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-nytimes_2009.feb.08-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> who intended the puzzles to be an instruction-free method of training the brain.<sup id="cite_ref-times_2008.march.21_2-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-times_2008.march.21-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The name derives from the [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "Japanese language") word for cleverness (賢, _ken, kashiko(i)_).<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_2009.feb.08_1-1"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-nytimes_2009.feb.08-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The names **Calcudoku** and **Mathdoku** are sometimes used by those who do not have the rights to use the KenKen or KenDoku trademarks.<sup id="cite_ref-3"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup>

As in [Sudoku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudoku "Sudoku"), the goal of each puzzle is to fill a grid with digits –– 1 through 4 for a 4×4 grid, 1 through 5 for a 5×5, 1 through 6 for a 6×6, etc. –– so that no digit appears more than once in any row or any column (a [Latin square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_square "Latin square")). Grids range in size from 3×3 to 9×9. Additionally, KenKen grids are divided into heavily outlined groups of cells –– often called “cages” –– and the numbers in the cells of each cage must produce a certain “target” number when combined using a specified mathematical operation (one of addition, [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction"), multiplication or division). For example, a linear three-cell cage specifying addition and a target number of 6 in a 4×4 puzzle must be satisfied with the digits 1, 2, and 3. Digits may be repeated within a cage, as long as they are not in the same row or column. No operation is relevant for a single-cell cage: placing the "target" in the cell is the only possibility (thus being a "free space"). The target number and operation appear in the upper left-hand corner of the cage.

In the English-language KenKen books of [Will Shortz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Will_Shortz "Will Shortz"), the issue of the non-[associativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associativity "Associativity") of division and subtraction is addressed by restricting clues based on either of those operations to cages of only two cells in which the numbers may appear in any order. Hence if the target is 1 and the operation is - (subtraction) and the number choices are 2 and 3, possible answers are 2,3 or 3,2. Some puzzle authors have not done this and have published puzzles that use more than two cells for these operations.

In 2007, toy inventor Robert Fuhrer, owner of Nextoy and creator of Gator Golf, Crocodile Dentist, and dozens of other popular toys and games, encountered KenKen books published in Japan by the educational publisher [Gakken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gakken "Gakken") Co., Ltd. and titled "Kashikoku naru Puzzle" (賢くなるパズル, _Kashikoku naru pazuru_; lit. "to become smart puzzle").<sup id="cite_ref-times_2008.march.21_2-1"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-times_2008.march.21-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Fuhrer's company [Nextoy](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nextoy&action=edit&redlink=1 "Nextoy (page does not exist)"), [LLC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_liability_company "Limited liability company") (now holder of a trademark on "KenKen" and "KenDoku" as a name for brain-training puzzles) and chess International Master [David Levy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Levy_(chess_player) "David Levy (chess player)") helped bring the puzzles to the attention of Michael Harvey, an editor of _[The Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Times "The Times")_ (London).<sup id="cite_ref-The_Next_Sudoku?_5-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-The_Next_Sudoku?-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Harvey, impressed with what he calls its "depth and magnitude", arranged for publication of such puzzles, starting in March 2008, in _The Times_. Other papers, including the _[New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_Times "New York Times")_, followed suit. KenKen now appears in more than 200 newspapers in the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and worldwide. In 2014, KenKen signed an agreement with DTI, the software division of inflight entertainment expert Advanced Flight Alliance AG, and its parent company Global Eagle Entertainment, to provide KenKen on international flights.<sup id="cite_ref-6"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2015, KenKen partnered with German news organization _[Der Spiegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Der_Spiegel "Der Spiegel")_.<sup id="cite_ref-8"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The magazine, which is one of Europe's largest news publications, offers KenKen puzzles on their mobile app.<sup id="cite_ref-9"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> KenKen is also being used by over 30,000 teachers throughout the United States to teach math skills, [problem solving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_solving "Problem solving") techniques, logic, and [critical thinking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_thinking "Critical thinking").<sup id="cite_ref-10"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Today KenKen can be played online at [www.kenkenpuzzle.com](http://www.kenkenpuzzle.com/), as well as on its iOS, [Android](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Android_(operating_system) "Android (operating system)") and Kindle Fire apps.

Solution to the above problem.

The objective is to fill the grid in with the digits 1 through 6 so that:

-   Each row contains exactly one of each digit
-   Each column contains exactly one of each digit
-   Each bold-outlined group of cells is a cage containing digits which achieve the specified result using the specified mathematical operation: addition (+), subtraction (−), multiplication (×), and division (÷). (Unlike [Killer Sudoku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killer_Sudoku "Killer Sudoku"), digits may repeat within a cage.)

Some of the techniques from Sudoku and Killer Sudoku can be used here, but much of the process involves the listing of all the possible options and eliminating the options one by one as other information requires.

In the example here:

(sets of numbers such as "5,6" and "4,5" could appear in any order)

-   "11+" in the leftmost column can only be "5,6"
-   "2÷" in the top row must be one of "1,2", "2,4" or "3,6"
-   "20×" in the fourth column must be "4,5".
-   "6×" in the top right must be "1,1,2,3". Therefore, the two "1"s must be in separate columns, thus row 1 column 5 is a "1".
-   "30x" in the fourth row down must contain "5,6"
-   "240×" on the left side is one of "6,5,4,2" or "3,5,4,4". Either way the five must be in the upper right cell because we have "5,6" already in column 1, and "5,6" in row 4. Also, then the combination must be "3,5,4,4" because there is nowhere to put the 6, with the above criteria.

More complex KenKen problems are formed using the principles described above but omitting the symbols +, −, × and ÷, thus leaving them as yet another unknown to be determined. Other authors of puzzles include more complex operations, including exponentiation, modulus, and bit-wise operations. Ranges of values can be varied, such as including zero, or having negative values (e.g., -2 to +2 in a 5-by-5 square).

One of KenKen's main uses is in the classroom as a means of teaching basic arithmetic and logic skills. In 2009, KenKen LLC began the KenKen Classroom (KKCR) Program, which allowed teachers to subscribe to a weekly newsletter providing KenKen puzzle sets, as well as other brain teasers, and KenKen news. Today, over 25,000 educators are a part of the KKCR Program. In addition to KKCR, KenKen also helps educators organize their own KenKen clubs and tournaments in their schools. KenKen partners with educators to help students learn while having fun. KenKen has collaborated with the [National Council of Teachers of Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_of_Teachers_of_Mathematics "National Council of Teachers of Mathematics") (NCTM), to create a mobile app in order to help students develop their math and logic skills.<sup id="cite_ref-11"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> KenKen is featured on [Scholastic Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scholastic_Corporation "Scholastic Corporation")'s online sites Scholastic Math and Scholastic Dynamath,<sup id="cite_ref-12"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> as well as on The Math Forum @ Drexel.<sup id="cite_ref-14"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup>

Beginning in 2010, KenKen CEO Robert Fuhrer has organized yearly KenKen tournaments held at the Chappaqua Library in Chappaqua, New York. Tournaments are open to all ages and skill levels. In addition to the Chappaqua tournament in December, Fuhrer partnered with IBM to host an international tournament in May 2015. In 2016, five students in India, Gaurav Pandey, Neharika Rajesh, Visharad Srivastava, Devika Pillai and Kajal Agarwal, beat 75,000 competitors to secure a place in the International Round that was held in New York on 18th Dec 2016. In addition to the visitors, many local students ages 8–18 competed in the tournament. The tournament was won by eight-year-old Gaurav Pandey, who beat out competitors up to twice his age.<sup id="cite_ref-15"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KenKen#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2016, the United Arab Emirates also took part in KenKen International Championship, with over 6000 students from more than 60 schools participating. Over the past several years KenKen has expanded internationally and now runs national tournaments in multiple countries for students. There are three age groups and the student winners of each division attend the KenKen International Championship in New York. Student divisions include Sigma (Approximate age is 14 and up), Kappa (Approximate age 11-14), and Delta (Approximate age 11 and younger). There is also an adult division and a senior division for those over the age of 60.
